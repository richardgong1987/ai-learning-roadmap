## Lesson 12 numerical results reviewed

For:

\[
f(x)=x^2-8x
\]

rewrite:

\[
f(x)=(x-4)^2-16
\]

Therefore:

\[
x^*=4,\qquad f^*=-16
\]

For:

\[
f(x,y)=x^2+y^2-6x-4y
\]

rewrite:

\[
f(x,y)=(x-3)^2+(y-2)^2-13
\]

Therefore:

\[
(x^*,y^*)=(3,2),\qquad f^*=-13
\]

A portfolio variance calculation with:

\[
\Sigma=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix},\quad
w=\begin{bmatrix}0.5\\0.5\end{bmatrix}
\]

gave:

\[
w^T\Sigma w=2
\]

## Main takeaway

A convex quadratic optimization problem uses a curved objective to model squared error, risk, distance, or energy. The matrix `P` controls the curvature. When `P \succeq 0`, the objective is convex; when `P \succ 0`, the objective is strictly convex and normally has a unique optimum on a convex feasible set.
