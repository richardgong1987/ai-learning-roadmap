25. Covariance

Key Insight:
Covariance measures whether two variables move together or in opposite directions by averaging the product of their deviations from their means.

Concrete Example:
Study hours and exam score can have positive covariance. If study hours are below average and score is also below average, the deviation product is positive. If study hours are above average and score is also above average, the deviation product is also positive.

Main Formula:

\[
\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])]
\]

ML Connection:
Covariance helps machine learning understand relationships between features. It is especially important in PCA, where covariance is used to find directions of maximum variation in the data.

26. Bayes Theorem

Key Insight:
Bayes Theorem updates belief after observing new evidence. It explains why the probability of a cause after evidence, P(A | B), is different from the probability of evidence given the cause, P(B | A).

Concrete Example:
In a disease-test example, even if the test is 99% sensitive, a positive result may not mean a 99% chance of disease when the disease is rare. With 10,000 people, 100 diseased people produce about 99 true positives, while 9,900 healthy people with a 5% false-positive rate produce about 495 false positives. So the probability of disease given a positive test is 99 / 594, about 16.67%.

Main Formula:

\[
P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A)P(A)}{P(B)}
\]

ML Connection:
Bayes Theorem is used in probabilistic classification, spam filtering, diagnosis, and Naive Bayes models.
